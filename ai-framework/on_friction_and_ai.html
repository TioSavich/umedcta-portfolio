On Friction and AI

Theodore M. Savich — February 2026
Notes on artificial intelligence, the work of learning, and why struggle matters.

"Now is the time for philosophy."
— Dr. Fredrick Schmidt, September 11, 2001, turning off the television in a Philosophy of Mind classroom at Indiana University

I have been thinking about AI for a few years now, and I have been thinking about learning for most of my life. These notes are an attempt to write down what I think the relationship is between the two. They are not a manifesto, not a pitch, and not a policy proposal. They are a record of where my thinking stands as of February 2026, after using generative AI extensively in my own teaching, writing, creative works, and research—sometimes successfully, sometimes regretfully. I am writing them for myself, but I am comfortable sharing them because I think the questions are worth asking plainly.

Where this starts

I fell in love with learning as a sixteen-year-old reading Kierkegaard in Sycamore Hall at Indiana University, escaping Bloomington High School North twice a week with a brand new driver's license. That experience changed what I thought I was. It did so because it was hard. Kierkegaard is not easy reading for anyone, let alone a kid who mostly hated school. But the difficulty was the point. The frustration of not understanding gave way, eventually, to moments where something clicked—where the world became a little more recognizable and a little more mine. Those moments set the agenda for my life.

In the years since—teaching math in the attic of an electrical company in Rushville, building a tutoring center in Indianapolis, teaching high school in the west side of Indianapolis, and a decade in the Wright School of Education at IU—I have tried to create the conditions for those moments to happen for other people. I have also tried to understand why they happen at all.

Friction

The word I keep coming back to is friction. Growth happens in cycles: tension builds, reflection occurs, and then something gives way. The tension can come from a hard problem, a confusing reading, a conversation where you realize you were wrong. The release is the "aha" moment that teachers live for—when a student is suddenly empowered to act, to write the essay they have been dreading, to attempt the proof they thought was impossible, to reconsider a belief they assumed was settled.

Without friction, none of that happens. We need the struggle. We need the period of confusion where the world feels unbounded and nothing makes sense, because that confusion is what makes the resolution meaningful. A teacher who removes all the difficulty from a course is not being kind. They are robbing students of the experience that makes learning feel like it belongs to them.

I learned this as a teacher, but I learned it more deeply as a father. Raising twin seven-year-olds has taught me that my job is not to eliminate friction from their lives. It is to pay attention to which friction is productive and which is just noise. My daughters are short. Putting their dishes on the top shelf would introduce friction, but it would not help them grow. So I put the dishes in a lower cabinet. The friction that matters is the kind that meets them where they are and pushes them just far enough that they have to stretch.

This is what faculty do. They curate frictional experiences—hard problems, challenging readings, assignments that require thought—and they provide ways of talking about those experiences. A teacher looks at a student's work and says "not quite," and that single judgment cuts an overwhelming field of possibilities down to something the student can deepen rather than drown in. The faculty member decides which barriers are worth preserving and which are just in the way.

What AI does

AI does the opposite of what good teaching does. It opens fields of possibility with extraordinary speed. It lets non-coders build applications. It generates a hundred variations of an assignment. It produces text at scales no human could match. But it does not know how to eliminate. It generates; it does not digest.

This is not a criticism. The ability to rapidly expand what is possible is genuinely exciting, and I have used it to solve real problems. But it is a description of a fundamental tension. The work of learning is, in part, the work of narrowing down—of taking the buzzing confusion of raw experience and carving it into something you can act on. Understanding, in the pragmatist tradition I was trained in, means knowing what to do next. When AI hands you the answer, you arrive somewhere, but you have no idea how to get home. It is like flying to the moon on a rocket ship: thrilling, but you are stranded if anything goes wrong, because you skipped the journey that would have taught you the terrain.

The companies selling AI products talk constantly about efficiency. In business contexts, that framing makes sense. In higher education, it is dangerous. Efficiency in learning often means skipping the part that matters. Using ChatGPT to write an essay is extremely efficient. It is also the educational equivalent of joining a fraternity for access to the file cabinet of old exams. Neither is a moral failing—both are strategic responses to a learning environment that has not given the student a reason to do the work. The interesting question is not whether students cheat. It is whether faculty have the support to design learning experiences that make cheating pointless.

What AI is, technically

I am not a computer scientist. But I study both human and machine computation. My research models the arithmetic strategies children invent—the informal algorithms they develop before anyone teaches them the "right" way—as formal mathematical objects called automata. The transducers at the heart of generative AI systems are, mathematically, close cousins of the models I use to describe how a seven-year-old figures out that 8 + 5 = 13 by counting on from 8.

The difference is in the training. The current generation of large language models is trained using algorithms that are, in their logic, behaviorist: the system learns patterns of association through reinforcement. This produces extraordinarily talented parrots—systems that can reproduce the surface structure of human thought with astonishing fluency. But they are not thinking. They do not make existential errors. They hallucinate, but they do not suffer as a result; they have no experience of confusion; they do not undergo the cycle of tension and release that constitutes genuine understanding. They can express words of regret, but cannot change their model weights in response to that awful feeling.

This matters because it tells us what these systems can and cannot do. They can handle pattern-matching, information retrieval, text transformation, and stylistic adaptation. They cannot determine whether a given piece of friction is productive or pointless. That judgment requires experience—the kind of experience that comes from years of watching students struggle, break down, and break through.

Where AI helps

I use AI regularly in my teaching and I find it genuinely useful—when it is in service of a specific pedagogical judgment I have already made.

When a student with dyscalculia nearly became physically ill encountering base systems in my math course, I vibe-coded an application that let her explore those systems without the anxiety of right and wrong answers. She needed more experience at the semi-concrete level of abstraction before she could generalize. I knew that because I am a teacher. I could not have built the app without AI because I do not have time to code from scratch for every student who needs a different entry point. The semi-concrete stage of abstraction is philosophically rather fascinating. What is a singular counting cube? A thing? An idea? Both, though the movement between those conceptualizations of concepts is where things get really interesting to me.

When a lecture on pluralism in a social studies methods course failed because students could not get past the political valence of the examples, I built a website that taught the same logical structure through geometry. The idea was mine—the recognition that the logical structure of pluralism can be demonstrated with quadrilaterals, that students might engage with the concept more openly when it is not wrapped in partisan politics. AI let me get that idea into a shareable form in fifteen minutes instead of a semester.

I use NotebookLM to generate reading accountability quizzes—a small psychological incentive that is sufficient for many students to crack the books. Humans are strategic thinkers; we avoid boring and repetitive tasks unless given a reason not to. I also use NotebookLM to craft discussion questions that surface paradoxes in the readings. My training in formal logic tells me that any meta-vocabulary—any text that talks about teaching, or about values, or about how to think—will contain paradoxes, because inclusion paradoxes are a structural feature of self-referential systems. AI helps me find the specific paradoxes in a specific text. The result: students who did the reading can share their expertise on what they learned; students who did not can still engage with the main themes without feeling stupid. Discussions have been much more lively this semester.

When I inherited a math methods course that had accumulated a decade of layered PowerPoints, Canvas modules, Google Docs, and publisher resources from multiple coordinators, I used NotebookLM to glean the gist of those accumulated materials so I could spend my preparation time designing genuine learning experiences rather than excavating digital sediment.

I have used IU's Big Red supercomputer to run next-gen OCR models to make my Canvas site ADA-compliant—converting scanned documents into machine-readable text that screen readers can actually work with.

Each of these was a judgment call made by a teacher who understood the specific need. None of them would have worked if the AI had been making the pedagogical decisions.

Where AI does not help

I tried using AI to write the first draft of my book. It was a mistake. At the end of the process, the book did not feel like it was mine. The "mineness" of creative work—the sense that what you have produced bears the marks of your own struggle with the material—is not a luxury. It is the thing that makes the work worth doing. AI challenges the ownership of experience. A song prompted into existence is not the same as one toiled over with pen and guitar. Neither is better or worse in some absolute sense, but they are different experiences for the person who made them, and that difference matters.

This connects to what I think is the deepest issue AI raises for education. Knowledge belongs to no one. But for students to feel invested in their learning, the experience of learning—the friction, the confusion, the eventual clarity—has to feel like theirs. When AI mediates too much of that process, students arrive at understanding without the experiential history that would make it meaningful. They can state the conclusion but cannot reconstruct the path. In pragmatist terms, they cannot act, because they do not know what comes next.

The digital drudgery problem

There is a different category of frustration that has nothing to do with productive friction: the sheer drudgery of maintaining a digitized curriculum. Broken Canvas links. Inaccessible videos. Ten versions of the same PowerPoint scattered across OneDrive. Publisher resources that require a login, a different login, and a third login. Trying to find the right file is like saying your ABCs backwards while counting to a hundred.

This is a problem that digitization created and that AI is uniquely suited to solve. These tasks are menial, demoralizing, high-stakes, and prone to error. They are not part of the essential curriculum for becoming a faculty member. Fixing a broken link is not a growth experience. Technological solutions to these problems would be welcomed with open arms, and developing and disseminating such tools is exactly the kind of work a center for teaching and learning should be doing.

But there is a wrinkle. AI will make the waste-management problem worse before it makes it better. Computers have no built-in way to automatically remove waste. Vibe-coding is incredible if the application can be done in one sitting. If new features are needed, the lack of waste removal quickly overwhelms the context windows of the AI systems that do the work. Without version control, without curation, without someone deciding what to keep and what to throw away, we will drown in digital sediment. The problem of digital clutter is not going to be solved by adding more digital tools. It will be solved by human judgment aided by tools that are specifically designed to reduce clutter rather than produce it.

What I think the right questions are

Not: Should we use AI? We are already using it, and some of those uses are genuinely good.

Not: How do we stop students from cheating with AI? Cheating is a strategic response to a poorly designed learning environment, not a moral failing. The answer is better design, not better surveillance.

Not: How do we make teaching more efficient? Efficiency is the wrong frame for education. We are not trying to produce outputs. We are trying to produce experiences that change people.

The right questions:

Which frictions are productive? Faculty know the answer to this for their disciplines. A historian knows which primary sources are worth struggling through. A mathematician knows which proofs require suffering. A writing instructor knows which drafts need to be terrible before they can be good. These people need to be trusted.

Which frictions are pointless? Broken links. Incompatible file formats. Ten versions of slides with no way to know which one was the latest. ADA compliance backlogs. The menial, demoralizing, error-prone work that eats into the time faculty should be spending on the work that matters. AI can help with these, and should.

Who decides? Faculty. Not administrators, not vendors, not the latest industry report. Innovation without faculty buy-in is not innovation—it is imposition. Shared governance over curricular technology is not an obstacle to progress; it is the mechanism that makes progress sustainable.

What I do not know

I do not know how the large-scale deployment of ChatGPT Edu to 120,000 users will play out. I do not know whether the Lilly Endowment's planning grant will produce recommendations that faculty find useful or that end up gathering dust. I do not know how the adoption of the Chicago Principles will change what can be taught and how. I do not know whether the text-to-speech algorithms that are currently impressive will stay impressive or be replaced by something I cannot predict.

What I do know is that the cycle of tension and release that defines genuine learning has survived every previous technological disruption—the printing press, the calculator, the internet, the smartphone. It will survive this one too, if the people making decisions about technology in higher education remember why the institution exists in the first place.

We are here to create the conditions under which people can change who they are by struggling with hard ideas. Everything else is logistics.
