UMEDCTA — Tio Savich

Understanding Mathematics as an Emancipatory Discipline: A Critical Theory Approach

Interactive tools companion to the manuscript UMEDCTA

These are experiments in breaking. Each page models something — a reasoning strategy, a physical system, a logical typology — and each is designed to be pushed until the model stops working. That stopping point is not incidental. The central argument of the accompanying manuscript is that formalisms productively exceed themselves when applied consistently: the breakdown is where the interesting philosophy begins.

The modeling is honest about what it leaves out. A Prolog formalization of a child's arithmetic strategy is not a theory of mathematical cognition; a Zeeman catastrophe machine running in a browser is not a metaphysics of embodied compression. What these tools do: make it possible to notice where the formalization stops — and what that stopping tells you about what the formalism was actually doing.

The tools accompany a manuscript rooted in Robert Brandom's analytic pragmatism, Hegel's dialectics of determinate negation, and Phil Carspecken's critical ethnography. The Prolog and Python formalization work is not included here; that material is research in progress, and claiming it "works" in any philosophically significant sense would be premature.

Children's Mathematical Reasoning

The Hermeneutic Calculator
Each button opens a page modeling one formal reasoning strategy from research on children's arithmetic. The strategies are drawn from Susan Jo Russell's work: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. The formalization makes explicit what a child's reasoning procedure commits to inferentially — and where those commitments run out. For those building AI in mathematics education: this is what an AI tutoring system currently lacks — the inferential structure of a child's procedure, made explicit enough to break.
Ace of Bases
A base system explorer. Users experiment with number representation in bases other than ten. The goal is not to teach base conversion but to make the contingency of base-ten notation available for examination — a starting point for understanding what a number system commits you to.

AI in Education

A Framework for AI in Education
Three modes of AI use in classroom settings — distinguished by whether AI takes a role in the conversation or produces content for students to engage with. The framework draws on Habermas's distinction between communicative and strategic action. The framework describes a teacher's options, not a theory of learning.
On Friction and AI
An essay on what productive difficulty does in learning, and what AI removes when it removes difficulty. Built through conversation with AI; the ideas are the author's, the implementation is not. The essay describes how this was made and what that process can and cannot mean for questions of authorship.
The Classroom Web
A network visualization of how mathematical ideas connect across classroom discussions. Students and their contributions appear as nodes; the connections track inferential dependencies rather than conversational sequence.

Types of Difference

Ten Types of Difference
An interactive guide to Brandom's reading of Hegel's Perception chapter. Ten logical structures of difference, rendered through geometry and equity examples. The geometry is not a metaphor for the logic — it instantiates the same inferential relations. The equity framing came from a class on pluralism where geometric examples worked where political ones did not.
Quadrilateral Substitution
A worked demonstration of Brandom's substitution inference in Chapter 4 of Articulating Reasons, using quadrilaterals. Substitution inference — the move from "this is a square" to "this is a rectangle" — turns out to require inferential commitment, not just conceptual inclusion. The geometry makes the commitment structure available to examination.
Five Practices for Productive Math Discussions
An interactive demonstration of the Smith & Stein pedagogical framework — anticipating, monitoring, selecting, sequencing, and connecting student thinking. The tool is designed to help teacher candidates recognize the framework's inferential logic, not to train them in it.

The Zeeman Catastrophe Machine

More Zeeman
A model of the Zeeman catastrophe machine — a physical device with two elastic bands attached to a rotating disc, where the control point can be dragged into a region where the disc snaps discontinuously between two stable positions. In the manuscript's appendix, the machine serves as a model for embodied compression and relaxation: the moment before a snap, the system is in a superimposed state; after, it has resolved — but the resolution carries information about the path. The "sound of time" visualizer registers angular velocity as acoustic displacement, so you can hear the snap. The matrix automaton in the corner grows by Cantorian diagonalization each time a catastrophe occurs.
